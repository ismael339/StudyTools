How to Use the Pomodoro Technique to Study Better

If you've ever sat down to study and found yourself scrolling through your phone 10 minutes later, the Pomodoro Technique might be the most practical solution you haven't tried properly yet.

What is the Pomodoro Technique? Work for 25 minutes without interruption, then take a 5-minute break. After 4 sessions, take a longer 15–30 minute break. Repeat.

Why it works for students

The human brain struggles to maintain deep focus for more than 25–45 minutes at a stretch. The Pomodoro method works with your biology rather than against it. By committing to only 25 minutes at a time, the task feels less overwhelming and your brain doesn't enter "avoidance mode."

Research from the University of Illinois found that brief mental breaks actually help maintain focus over longer periods — exactly what Pomodoro delivers.

How to use it correctly

  1. Choose one specific task before starting the timer — not "study math" but "complete exercises 5–12 from chapter 3."
  2. Set the timer for 25 minutes and work without any interruptions.
  3. When the timer rings, take a 5-minute break — stand up, stretch, drink water. No scrolling.
  4. After 4 pomodoros, take a longer break of 15–30 minutes.
  5. Track your pomodoros to understand how many you need for each subject.

Common mistakes students make

Checking the phone during work time. This completely breaks the technique. Put your phone face-down or in another room during each 25-minute block.

Working through breaks. The break is not optional — it's what makes the next session productive. Use it to physically move away from your desk.

Using too many sessions without a long break. After 4 pomodoros your brain needs real rest. Don't skip the long break or your concentration will drop sharply.

Adjusting the intervals

The classic 25/5 split works well for most students, but some people find 50/10 more effective for deep work like writing essays or solving complex problems. Experiment and find what keeps you in flow.
